    The Expanse

    Jonathan Hartley

    2022-04-08 14:42

    (This post rescues content from a Facebook comment I got carried away yesterday.)

    There's so much to write about the Expanse. It's such a relief to find a science fiction show with a futuristic setting that is vastly different from our current day-to-day reality, depicted in a way that doesn't make scientifically literate people cringe and shout at the television.

    a wonderful believable depiction of an entirely plausible future, complete with characters, politics, in futuristic setting that

    On many axes, it's the best SF on television, which is saying something, as there are so many competitors. Science fiction output is following some sort of Moore's law of endless expansion. But this is a mixed blessing, because the resulting shows & movies are often failing to address the psychological need that prompted their creation. And in some ways, the Expanse is a prime example of this.

    My SF podcast guy defines SF as the arena in which we forge new myths of psychological meaning, which are sorely needed as traditional myths, based on nature and superstition, dwindle in relevance to modern life. The movements of spiritual atheism and the like are a reflection that even the least spiritual amongst us recognize that something is missing.

    So. We use SF to construct mythic narratives, striving against humanity's current frontiers, to craft tales of personal trials and transformations using our versions of gods and magic - which is very evidently now science and technology. The label "science fiction" is a misnomer, as this style of fiction has existenced long before science was the focus, and it naturally gravitated towards using science as it became more apparent that science is now where society's power and transformation springs from, and the name "science fiction" was then applied post-facto, missing the underlying fact that this is just mythic fiction with some modern attributes.

    The Expanse, like many other shows, taps into this desire to utilize the trappings of SF, and benefits from taking the time for quiet moments of character work, from Miller's lonely poignant realization in series 01 that he wasn't the office maveric

    coupled with a peerlessly "realistic" depiction of a futuristic SF setting that doesn't make scientifically literate watchers cringe. But it does so without fulfilling its deeper, more mythical or symbolic obligations.
